Rosemary oil has been used in traditional medicine for centuries. But does science actually support using it for hair growth?

The short answer is promising, with important caveats. A small but growing body of clinical research suggests rosemary oil may stimulate hair growth through mechanisms similar to minoxidil, the only FDA-approved topical hair growth treatment.

## The Key Clinical Trial

The most cited study was published in SKINmed Journal in 2015. Researchers compared rosemary oil to 2% minoxidil in 100 patients with androgenetic alopecia over six months.

Both groups showed significant increases in hair count at the six-month mark. The rosemary group actually had fewer complaints of scalp itching, a common minoxidil side effect.

## How Rosemary Oil Works

Rosemary oil appears to promote hair growth through several mechanisms. It increases blood circulation to the scalp when applied topically, delivering more nutrients and oxygen to hair follicles.

The oil also contains carnosic acid, which has been shown to help regenerate damaged nerves and tissue. In the context of hair growth, this may help revitalize follicles that have become miniaturized.

Additionally, rosemary oil has anti-inflammatory properties that can reduce scalp inflammation, a contributing factor in several types of hair loss.

## How to Use It Safely

The most important rule is never apply undiluted essential oil to your scalp. Always dilute rosemary oil in a carrier oil at a ratio of 3-5 drops per tablespoon.

Apply the mixture to your scalp and massage gently for 2-3 minutes. Leave it on for at least 30 minutes before washing out. For best results, use this treatment 2-3 times per week.

## Realistic Expectations

Rosemary oil is not a miracle cure. The existing research is encouraging but limited in scope. Most studies involve small sample sizes and relatively short follow-up periods.

If you're experiencing significant hair loss, rosemary oil may be worth trying as part of a comprehensive approach, but it shouldn't replace consultation with a dermatologist who can identify and treat the underlying cause.
